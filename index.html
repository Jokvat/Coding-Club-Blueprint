<Blueprint>

Club Vision:

The vision for the Coding Club is to connect high school students who have an interest in learning to program, and to use those connections to build a welcoming community in which everyone strengthens each others' coding skills. It would be accessible to anyone with any skill level—whether they haven't written a single line of code, or they designed and programmed an entire video game; however, the primary focus is to introduce beginners into the world of coding using web development, as there are many free and wonderful resources available. Although the focus is on beginners, anyone with experience who may be interested in a programming community are encouraged to join for opportunities to gain leadership experience, engage with fellow members who share the same passion, and to help others with their interest in coding; as it is said, teaching is the best way to learn.

Learning to program can seem like a very daunting process, and it is easy to get overwhelmed with the vast amounts of information available on the internet. Having an encouraging community that strives to help each other, and a sense of direction are both important factors that contribute to learning, all of which would be provided by the Coding Club.

Additionally, hosting events—taking place online or in person—is another goal for the club. These events could further introduce students of any age to discover that they can build these "intimidating" programming skills.

Resources:

I believe following a similar style to a book club, where a certain portion of a chosen resource (Book/Course/Curriculum) is assigned before each club session. This allows for more time during club meetings to discuss, and work on fun, hands-on projects. I don't wish for the club to become another "chore" or "class," so nothing would be too heavy, especially considering the fact that everyone has their academics to prioritize.

Some resources that could be used for this "book club" style of learning:

  • Code Academy
  • Code.org
  • The Odin Project - I personally have been following the Odin Project's curriculum for a few months now, and I have been really enjoying it.
  • Free Code Camp
  • Edabit
  • There are many more resources available, but these are some that I have found to be the most popular.

Considerations

A main concern of mine was how the club would be run, specifically addressing the session system. I wanted to consider any new members that may join, in addition to any returning members, and I did not want them to feel lost.

An idea was proposed to me, however, that there should be additional breakout rooms at the beginning of every session, allowing new club members to not be left out. I thought that the idea was wonderful, and I believe it could be a perfect fit for the Coding Club.

Communication

Meetings could be held via Google Meet or Zoom, as they are the most commonly used online meeting applications. Also, the club could utilize platforms including Discord or Slack to communicate outside of club meetings, including asking for help or guidance and searching for additional resources.

Collaboration:

Collaboration is important when learning how to program, and I have researched and gathered some resources that can contribute to this key aspect of coding:

  • GitHub - Web platform that allows collaboration and sharing of code using Git, a version control system.
  • Glitch - Web platform used for collaboratively building web applications with real time updates.
  • CodeSandbox - "Code sandbox gives you 24/7 collaborative cloud development environments that resume in 2 seconds." Includes sandboxes allowing for secure, shareable code.
  • Code Pen - Code pen's collaborative mode is locked behind a subscription, however I included because it is still great for testing out web development.
  • Once again, there are many more platforms and applications available

Extra:

I also have a couple ideas that could be potentially implemented: guest speakers, and session projects. Guest speakers would be a beneficial addition to the club meetings, helping showcase how programming can be applied in various professions, and inspiration as well. Regarding the session projects, each activity done in every meeting could be a smaller part of an overall project, slowly sticking each piece together. Instead of discarding the small tasks that would be completed each meeting, putting them all together to form a working website, for example, would give a sense of accomplishment. Additionally, all the activities completed in each meeting would be put to use, giving purpose to them, rather than just throwing them away. Overall, I believe both additions would be great to include.
